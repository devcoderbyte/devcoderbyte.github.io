# Escalation Framework

The decision tree I use to determine when to escalate, what to escalate, and how to frame it. Built from 10+ years of managing high-stakes client relationships where the wrong escalation — or a missing one — can define an account.

---

## The Core Principle

Escalation is not failure. The failure is escalating too late, escalating the wrong thing, or escalating without a proposed path forward.

> Never escalate a problem. Always escalate a situation with a recommended response.

---

## When to Escalate

**Escalate immediately when:**
- A P1 incident is unresolved at the 2-hour mark
- A client stakeholder raises a concern that isn't in the current risk register
- A delivery milestone will be missed and the client doesn't know yet
- A vendor has failed a committed dependency and it impacts the client
- A team member flags a risk that changes the delivery picture

**Do not escalate when:**
- You have enough information and authority to resolve it yourself
- The issue is on track to resolve within SLA without intervention
- You're escalating to avoid ownership — escalation is not delegation

---

## What to Escalate — The One-Page Format

Every escalation I raise follows this structure. One page, no exceptions:

**Situation:** What happened, when, and what's the current status. Two sentences maximum.

**Impact:** What is the client impact — SLA, revenue, relationship. Be specific. "May cause issues" is not an impact statement.

**Root cause (if known):** What caused it. If unknown, say so — and say when you'll know.

**Options:** Two or three response paths, with pros and cons for each. Never escalate without options.

**Recommendation:** Which option I recommend and why. Make it easy for leadership to decide.

**What I need:** Specifically what decision or resource I need from the escalation recipient. One clear ask.

## Escalation Paths by Scenario

| Scenario | First escalation | If unresolved |
|---|---|---|
| P1 unresolved at 2hrs | Delivery Manager | CTO / VP Engineering at 4hrs |
| Client relationship risk | Account Manager + Delivery Manager | Executive sponsor at 48hrs |
| Vendor failure | Vendor escalation contact | Legal/commercial review if repeated |
| Scope dispute | PMO + Account Manager | Contract review |
| Team capacity risk | Delivery Manager | Resource planning immediately |

---

## How to Frame an Escalation to a Client

**Rule 1:** Never surprise a client with bad news in a meeting. Call or message before the formal update.

**Rule 2:** Lead with what you're doing, not what went wrong. "We've identified an issue and here's our response plan" lands better than "we have a problem."

**Rule 3:** Give them one thing to decide or approve — not a list of concerns.

**Rule 4:** Follow up in writing within 2 hours of any verbal escalation. Creates a paper trail and demonstrates control.

**Rule 5:** After resolution, send a brief "closed" note — what happened, what was fixed, what prevents recurrence. Clients remember how you closed things as much as how you handled them.
