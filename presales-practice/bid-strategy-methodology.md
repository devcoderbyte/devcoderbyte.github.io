# Bid Strategy Methodology

Five questions I answer before writing a single word of any bid response. This framework shifted our proposals from capability-led to problem-led — and drove a 25% improvement in conversion rate across global bids.

---

## Why Most Bids Lose Before They're Read

Most RFQ responses lead with the vendor. Company overview. Awards. Global presence. Team size.

The client doesn't care. They have a problem. They want to know if you understand it and whether you can fix it.

The bids that win lead with the client.

---

## The Five Questions

### Question 1 — What is the client's real problem?

Not what's in the RFQ. The RFQ describes symptoms. The real problem is what's keeping the client's leadership up at night.

**How to find it:**
- Discovery call notes — what did they say unprompted?
- Past ticket or escalation patterns if we have access
- Industry context — what are their peers struggling with right now?
- What they asked about pricing tells you what they're worried about

**Output:** One sentence. "The client's real problem is \_\_\_."

---

### Question 2 — What does winning look like for them?

Define their success metric before defining our solution. If we don't know what winning looks like for the client, we can't design a proposal that gets them there.

**How to define it:**
- Ask directly in discovery: "What would make this engagement a clear success in 12 months?"
- If they don't have an answer, help them build one — that's a differentiator in itself
- Align every section of the proposal to their success metric

**Output:** "They will know this worked when \_\_\_."

---

### Question 3 — Where are we uniquely credible?

Lead with domain proof, not capability lists. Generic credentials don't win bids. Specific, relevant experience does.

**How to identify it:**
- What have we done that is closest to what they need?
- Can we name a client in the same domain? (Even without naming them explicitly)
- What outcome can we point to with a number?

**Output:** Two proof points maximum. Specific, relevant, quantified.

---

### Question 4 — What are the top 3 risks — and our answers?

Name risks before the client does. A proposal that acknowledges risk and answers it builds more trust than one that presents a frictionless fantasy.

**Standard risks to address:**
- Transition risk (if displacing a current vendor)
- Scope evolution risk (almost always happens)
- Knowledge dependency risk (key people risk)

**Format:** Risk → Likelihood → Our mitigation → Why this works.

---

### Question 5 — What's the one thing they'll remember?

Every winning bid has a single memorable hook. It's the thing the evaluator says to their colleague when summarising the options.

**How to find it:**
- It's usually at the intersection of their biggest pain and our clearest proof point
- It should be expressible in one sentence
- It should appear in the executive summary and echo at the close

**Output:** One sentence. If you can't say it in one sentence, it's not a hook — it's a feature list.

---

## The Sequence

1. Answer all five questions **before** opening a document
2. Write the executive summary **first** — the hook, the problem, the win condition
3. Build the rest of the proposal to support the executive summary
4. Review: does every section answer "so what?" from the client's perspective?
5. Final check: read only the first and last paragraph. Does the story hold?
